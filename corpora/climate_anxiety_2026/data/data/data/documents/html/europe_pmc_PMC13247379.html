Ecological Emotions in a Climate-Vulnerable Nation: Eco-Anxiety, Eco-Guilt, and Eco-Grief Among Gen Z Filipinos

Figures: 1 | Tables: 5 | Source: europe_pmc_PMC13247379.xml

Abstract

Ongoing global environmental change has given rise to a growing body of research on emotional and affective responses to ecological degradation. This study investigated eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief among Generation Z Filipinos, thereby contributing to the Global South perspective in a literature dominated by Western samples. Using a mixed-methods sequential explanatory (QUAN → qual) design, Study 1 employed a cross-sectional survey of 616 Gen Z Filipinos (aged 18–29) using the Eco-Anxiety Questionnaire, Eco-Guilt Questionnaire, and Ecological Grief Questionnaire. Study 2 involved qualitative interviews with nine participants. Quantitative findings revealed that participants exhibited high levels of habitual ecological worry, eco-guilt, and eco-grief, but relatively low functional impairment from eco-anxiety. Significant gender differences emerged, with females reporting higher levels of habitual ecological worry, eco-guilt, and eco-grief than males, although both genders experienced similar negative consequences. Place of residence also mattered: rural residents reported significantly greater negative consequences from eco-anxiety than urban residents, despite comparable levels of ecological worry, guilt, and grief. Qualitative analysis identified five themes—Gendered Emotional Expression, Responsibility Rooted in Nurturing Roles, Emotional Resonance and Collective Grief, Proximity to Environmental Harm,Disruption of Tradition and Place Identity and —that illuminate how gender socialization and environmental exposure shape ecological emotions. Our preliminary empirical insights and findings highlight the importance of recognizing ecological emotions as legitimate psychological phenomena warranting further investigations and attention in mental health practice and environmental education, particularly among climate-vulnerable youth populations.

Introduction

The escalating ecological crisis, marked by interconnected issues such as climate change, biodiversity loss, and environmental degradation, poses a severe global threat. 1 2 3 4 This has been described by the United Nations Secretary-General, António Guterres, as a “code red for humanity”. Importantly, the global ecological crisis is generating profound psychological impacts and giving rise to new forms of environmental distress including feelings of grief, loss, and frustration. New terminology is being rapidly developed to capture these specific psychological responses to environmental degradation. Associated with this effort is a burgeoning literature on “ecological emotions”, which refer to the emotional impact of environmental degradation and climate change and which are often described using terms associated with psychopathology. Some of the most prominent constructs in this emerging area of research are: “eco-anxiety,” “ecological guilt,” and “environmental grief.”

Within the Affective-Existential Framework of Climate Distress, eco-anxiety, ecological guilt, and environmental grief are understood as central emotional processes that both constrain and motivate human adaptation. 5 Rather than viewing climate-related distress as a purely clinical fear response, this framework conceptualizes it as an existential threat to core dimensions of human life—mortality, meaning, isolation, and freedom—thereby giving rise to a range of ecological emotions, including fear, anger, grief, and hope.

While cognitive reactions measure understanding and behavioral reactions measure outward action, they often fail to capture the “internalized crisis” that determines long-term psychological resilience. 3 6 7 8911 These three variables are uniquely sensitive to social-ecological positioning: environmental grief captures the loss of place-based identity, ecological guilt captures the conflict of modern agency, and eco-anxiety captures the anticipatory dread of systemic collapse. Moreover, these constructs are fundamentally psychological reactions to the ecological crisis, marked by corresponding emotional states that are subsequently interconnected with cognitive patterns and behavioral manifestations. As such, highlighting eco-emotions allows for specificity in relation to the ecological context, marking its distinction from broader, encompassing psychological reactions.

Consequently, focusing on this triad provides a more comprehensive view of the ‘lived experience’ of the ecological crisis than a focus on purely cognitive or behavioral metrics. Thus, this present study seeks to comprehensively investigate and provide an in-depth understanding of the psychological responses of individuals in a climate-vulnerable nation to major ecological challenges by integrating quantitative and qualitative data using a mixed-methods design structured within a sequential explanatory framework.

Defining Anxiety, Guilt, and Grief in the Context of Ecological Crisis

Eco-anxiety is often used interchangeably with “climate anxiety”. However, eco-anxiety encompasses a range of adverse emotions, such as worry, fear, anger, and despair, in response to the ecological crisis,5712712 13 while climate anxiety specially captures anxiety regarding the climate crisis. It is also crucial to differentiate eco-anxiety, which can involve clinically significant suffering, from mild normative worry.

Alongside anxiety, researchers also explored feelings of guilt arising from the ecological crisis. “Ecological guilt” refers to the feeling of regret, a sense of accountability, and uneasiness when one recognizes their role in environmental damage, whether intentional or unintentional. 14 9915 9 Ágoston and colleagues found that eco-guilt was linked to collective guilt, thus, making people depend on group efforts to solve ecological issues. Furthermore, they mentioned that eco-guilt encompasses feelings of regret regarding personal actions and choices, such as the over-production of waste that causes harm to the natural world. Specific literature has identified some effects of eco-guilt on individuals. Ágoston and colleagues found that eco-guilt can stimulate personal responsibility and self-criticism, resulting in greater awareness of one’s influence on the environment. Thus, this cultivates a desire within the individual to modify their values and actions as guided by environmental principles.

Following eco-anxiety and eco-guilt, another related prominent emotion in the literature is ecological grief. “Ecological grief” refers to the perceived sense of loss in the environment or an anticipated loss in the future as a result of a deteriorating environment. 14 16 17 16 171416 Benham and Hoerst further elaborated that this ecological emotion is characterized by a sense of yearning, sorrow, and sadness in response to their environment’s past, present, or future ruination. These feelings could be manifested in a variety of contexts; thus, three types of eco-grief have been identified: grief related to physical and ecological loss, grief related to the loss of the environmental knowledge system and loss of identity, and grief associated with anticipated ecological loss. Eco-grief has been linked to acute events and chronic environmental losses. In addition, Rai mentioned that eco-grief was prevalent among individuals who have a close relationship with nature and the environment around them. Other studies support this observation, finding that many people possess place attachment and a sense of belongingness with their environment.

Locating the Global South in Ecological Emotions Research

Current research on ecological emotions remains limited by its focus on WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic) populations, neglecting a global context. This is a critical oversight, as the Global Environmental Crises (GEC) disproportionately impact impoverished and disadvantaged communities. A 2023 study by Voşki and colleagues in Turkey identified a pattern of distinct ecological emotions, driven by factors like environmental justice, developing country tension, self-efficacy, and responsibility attribution. 18 Contrary to the pervasive eco-anxiety found in WEIRD nations, their research found eco-anger to be the most prominent emotion among Turkish participants.

The disproportionate scholarly attention to global environmental challenges of the Global North represents a critical research gap, as the psychological impacts of ecological crises differ markedly for the Global South. For instance, Tsevreni and colleagues found that eco-anxiety varies in both degree and intensity between these regions. 12 19 Furthermore, Stafford and colleagues note that psychological struggles in the Global South are compounded by factors like political instability, conflict, and limited access to mental health services.

These compounded pressures create severe psychological distress and heightened vulnerability. A recent international poll 8 8 20 21 reflects this, showing that a large majority of young people in Global South nations, including the Philippines (92%), Brazil (86%), India (80%), and Nigeria (70%), report intense fear of climate change. Notably, this and other studies consistently identify anger, not anxiety, as the most prevalent ecological emotion in these contexts. Hickman and colleagues also reported anger as a primary response, a finding corroborated by Ogunbode et al. and supported by the qualitative work of Kleres and Wettergren on environmental activists.

Ecological Emotions in the Philippines

The Philippines is uniquely vulnerable to the impacts of climate change. As an archipelago, its geographical location and physical climate make it susceptible to a multitude of disasters caused by natural hazards, including typhoons, floods, earthquakes, and landslides. 222325 24 8 It is consistently ranked among the top three nations most affected by such events, facing an average of eight calamities and as many as 20 typhoons annually. This high level of exposure has positioned the country as being at significant risk from climate change. Consequently, an international poll by Hickman and colleagues found that the Philippines had the highest proportion of participants (92%) among Global South nations who feared the effects of climate change on their future.

This constant threat has a profound psychological impact, particularly on the younger generation. While Filipinos in disaster-prone areas show greater climate change awareness and engagement in adaptation, 24 12 24 12 they also develop more intense negative emotions. Studies show that Filipino youth experience heightened worry that affects their daily functioning. The Philippines has the highest rate of young people experiencing intense climate anxiety, as well as emotions like anger, hopelessness, and frustration. Tsevreni and colleagues strengthen this finding, reporting that 83% of the younger generation in the Philippines experience eco-anxiety, with 62.4% experiencing a particularly dark, intense, and impactful form of this affective response.

The focus on youth is critical globally as Generation Z, often called the “climate generation”, has spent their entire lives affected by environmental adversities. 12 26 272428 291214 30 Living through rapid climate change and ecosystem loss, this generation is considered one of the most predisposed to its adverse psychological effects. They hold a dark picture of the environmental future and express significant worry about the long-term effects of climate change they will inevitably experience. This distress manifests as ecological emotions; from 2010 to 2019, feelings of worry, anger, and guilt regarding climate change increased among younger generations. Current literature confirms a higher prevalence of eco-anxiety in young individuals, with Gen Z displaying strong feelings of guilt due to an inability to protect the environment. Furthermore, when their concerns are disregarded, it can aggravate feelings of isolation and distress, particularly for those who feel powerless to enact change.

The Current Study

Despite growing research on the psychological effects of the ecological crisis, significant gaps remain, especially in the Philippine context. There is a need for an extensive investigation focusing on the experiences of anxiety, guilt, and grief among Gen Z Filipinos. Most existing studies focus on a single emotion71215283133 7 28 28 and lack profound knowledge of how the interplay of these emotions affects the youth. Pihkala also identified a scarcity of studies on the different manifestations of anxiety and their relationship with other psychological impacts. Furthermore, in spite of the repeated occurrence of disasters caused by natural hazards in the Philippines, overall public awareness of climate change remains low. This study aims to address these gaps by supplying more specific information and exploring how emotional responses to the ecological crisis relate to demographic variables, such as regional residence, and mental health, as suggested by Reyes and colleagues.

Our objective is supported by the theory of Ontological Security, introduced by psychiatrist R.D. Laing in 1960 and reinvented by sociologist Anthony Giddens in 1991. This theory emphasizes that feelings of security or insecurity deeply influence an individual’s well-being and behavior, supporting the claim that environmental problems can result in ontological insecurity that develops into anxiety, depression, or defensive emotions. 7 34 28 26 This study focused on Gen Z Filipinos, born between 1995 and 2010 and aged 18 to 29, as this generation exhibits significant stress about future concerns like climate change and is highly vulnerable to its consequences despite negligible contribution to causing the crisis.

Moreover, testing gender differences was necessary because previous literature (e.g., Clayton, 32 6 35 32 363738) suggests women often carry a disproportionate emotional burden regarding climate change. Thus, the present study aimed to see if this Global North trend held true in a Global South context like the Philippines. Likewise, gender (i.e., male and female) and location (i.e., urban and rural) are key variables for understanding eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief. To understand why demographic and geographic factors are not just ‘check-boxes’ but core drivers of environmental emotion, we have to look at the Social-Ecological Vulnerability Framework. This framework suggests that eco-anxiety, guilt, and grief are not distributed equally because our social positions dictate how much we have to lose and how much responsibility we feel forced to carry. We cannot treat environmental distress as a ‘one-size-fits-all’ clinical issue. It is a rational response to one’s specific social and geographical reality. This framework illustrates how individual vulnerability is not static but is nested within micro (family/local environment), meso (community), and macro (culture/government) systems that dictate emotional responses to environmental change.

To delve into their psychological responses, we employed a mixed-methods sequential explanatory approach (QUAN → qual), responding to the call by Tsevreni and colleagues 12 to explore eco-anxiety through both quantitative and qualitative research in climate-affected countries. Using a sequential mixed-methods design, the study first conducted a descriptive, cross-sectional quantitative assessment of ecological emotions, followed by a descriptive qualitative inquiry aimed at contextualizing and elaborating on Gen Z Filipinos’ experiences of eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief.

Method

A mixed-methods sequential explanatory design (QUAN → qual) was utilized to investigate eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief among Generation Z Filipinos. Quantitative data were collected and analyzed first in Study 1, followed by a qualitative inquiry intended to contextualize and deepen interpretation of the quantitative results in Study 2.

The present investigation secured ethics approval from Davao de Oro State College – Research Ethics Committee (Protocol Code: 824-08-2024). We sought participants who are Filipinos belonging to Gen Z, aged 18 to 29 years old. Participants who were experiencing psychological distress or had a diagnosed psychological condition at the time of data collection were excluded from the study. This criterion was applied to ensure that reported ecological emotions (i.e., anxiety, guilt, and grief) were attributable primarily to the ecological crisis rather than to pre-existing psychological vulnerabilities. Ethically, this decision also reflected the sensitive nature of the topic and its potential to exacerbate distress among individuals with psychological concerns.

Procedure

Study 1 used voluntary response sampling by disseminating the questionnaires through an accessible link, which was posted and shared across various social media platforms such as Facebook, Messenger, X, Instagram, and TikTok. Data collection was done using an online research questionnaire via Google Forms survey. This survey contained information on the nature and rationale of the study, demographic questions, informed consent, and the test battery (comprises EAQ-22, EGuiQ-11, & EGriQ-6). The three scales in the test battery were randomized in order using the website allocate.monster to minimize and control the possible effects of systematic order. Study 1 used the original English scales without translation or cultural modification. This decision was based on the high level of English proficiency in the Philippine academic and professional sectors. To further mitigate potential language barriers, only individuals who self-identified as having a fair understanding of English were eligible for inclusion in the study. While the original English versions of the instruments were utilized due to the high English proficiency of the target academic demographic, the internal reliability of these measures was closely monitored to ensure their validity. The resulting Cronbach’s alpha scores, which are comparable to or exceed those reported in the original validation study by Ágoston and colleagues, 5 serve as a form of sample-specific validation, indicating that the items were interpreted consistently by the participants. Upon completion of the online survey, participants received a detailed written debriefing alongside contact numbers for crisis hotlines (e.g., National Center for Mental Health hotline).

Data Analysis

The data gathered was subjected to descriptive and inferential statistical analysis. We used a web-based spreadsheet application Google Sheets (web version) and the statistical program Jamovi ver. 2.5.2 in performing the data analysis. Descriptive statistics (means and standard deviations) and independent-samples t tests were used in the present study. Welch’s t test was employed to account for unequal sample sizes, as it adjusts the degrees of freedom for unequal variances and provides strong control of Type I error. 50

Design

This method of gathering data ensures an extensive and detailed comprehension of the viewpoints held by Gen Z Filipinos on the ecological crisis and its emotional impacts, which, while typically exploratory in nature, it enables an in-depth examination of participants’ lived experiences and provides richer explanation and contextualization of the quantitative findings.

The qualitative sample was drawn from participants in Study 1 who completed the initial questionnaire and indicated their willingness to participate in a subsequent online interview. Each interview was held online via Zoom or Google Meet, depending on the participant’s preference and convenience. Each interview lasted approximately one hour and 30 minutes to two hours. This concept is integral not only in determining an adequate sample size but also contributes to the validity and credibility of qualitative research.

Data Analysis

Thematic analysis (TA) was used to understand experiences, thoughts, and behaviors across a data set. 60 61 Braun and Clark have identified six key steps in conducting TA. First, during the familiarization phase, we engaged and immersed ourselves in the data by thoroughly reading and rereading it to gain in-depth understanding. This is followed by the coding process, where we systematically assigned labels or codes to the significant aspects of the data, either through inductive reasoning or by utilizing a pre-existing coding framework. These initial codes were then organized into broader categories or themes by identifying the patterns and relationships present. We then reviewed and refined these themes to ensure that they accurately reflect the data. This was followed by defining and naming those themes, creating clear and distinct definitions for each. Here, we identified the essence of each theme and determined the specific aspects of data the theme captures. Finally, in the write-up phase, we provided a concise, coherent, logical, non-repetitive, and interesting report of the data, which ensured that the analysis was credible and valid.

Furthermore, we employed auditing and member checking as validation strategies to ensure the integrity of the qualitative analysis as well as to control possible confounding of results. Implementing an audit trail involves recording how a qualitative study was conducted and how the researchers arrived at their conclusions. 62 63 Member checking on the other hand, involved presenting each interviewee with their own data transcripts and analysis to gather feedback, which provided credibility to the interpretation.

Integrating reflexivity was also essential for our second phase analysis, given the potential subjectivity and biases that may surface when analyzing the data gathered from our participants. Reflexivity, as defined by Gouldner in 1971, is the analytical focus on the researcher’s role in qualitative research, which requires a high degree of self-awareness at every step of the research process. 64 65 64 666567 It involves analyzing one’s presumptions, beliefs, and judgments, and critically assessing how this influences the study. Through reflexivity, we assessed how our experiences change as a result of the research process and evaluate how these changes affect the study itself. Therefore, we recognized that the codes we input during TA represented our interpretations of the patterns seen in the dataset. We reflected on our identities as researchers and acknowledged how our values, life circumstances, social roles, and ideologies were shaped by the research we presented. Through this, we adopted a critical thinking approach that challenges us to examine the ‘whys’ and ‘hows’ of our research, questioning the utility, ethics, and value of what, who, and how we study.

After completing the quantitative investigation of the study, we moved on to the qualitative study to explore more deeply how Gen Z Filipinos understand and navigate their experiences of eco-emotions in relation to both their gender and residential context. The interview central question: How do Gen Z Filipinos experience and make sense of their eco-anxiety, eco-grief, and eco-guilt in the context of their gender and place of residence? in our qualitative Study 2 was then derived to sequentially explain these quantitative results.

Study 2: Qualitative

Nine participants provided informed consent to participate in Study 2. Of these, five resided in urban areas and four in rural areas; the sample comprised five males and four females. A gender imbalance was observed in Study 1, with females disproportionately represented in the quantitative sample. This imbalance was considered during the selection of participants for Study 2, and deliberate efforts were made to include representation from both genders among those who expressed willingness to participate in qualitative interviews. As a result, Study 2 achieved a nearly equal distribution of male and female participants, which served to partially mitigate the female-heavy composition of the initial sample. Although quantitative findings indicated higher levels of ecological worry, guilt, and grief among female participants, the qualitative analysis was designed to examine whether the nature and expression of these ecological emotions differed across genders, thereby ensuring that male participants’ perspectives were adequately captured and interpreted.

During the interviews, participants discussed how gender and place of residence shaped their emotional experiences and responses to the ecological crisis. The “ecological crisis,” while broad, serves as the unifying phenomenon through which our diverse participants navigate their emotions. Our goal was not to find a single experience but to see how the same crisis is “socially lived and emotionally shared” across different settings.

Participants across different gender identities and residential contexts generally expressed similar thoughts and feelings. This consistency reinforced the shared nature of their experiences and confirmed the decision not to create separate thematic distinctions for these non-significant variables. The focus was therefore directed toward themes that meaningfully expanded upon the study’s statistically significant findings.

To present a more cohesive narrative, the findings are organized by overarching themes rather than being separated strictly by gender or location. This approach emerged from the analysis, which revealed that themes were consistent across these demographics, often expressed through shared experiences or participants’ observations about others. While minor demographic patterns did surface, they are woven into the broader discussion of the main themes to maintain clarity and present a unified picture of how Gen Z Filipinos experience and express ecological emotions. Moreover, the findings of the qualitative sample, drawn from the personal narratives of the selected participants, are interpreted as patterns, tendencies, or suggestibility.

Thematic Findings Based on Gender Differences

From the qualitative data, three central themes emerged related to how Gen Z participants experience eco-emotions through the lens of gender. These themes provide depth to the statistically significant findings from the quantitative study, particularly where female participants scored significantly higher in habitual ecological worry, eco-grief, and eco-guilt compared to males.

Theme 1: Gendered Emotional Expression

This theme highlights the perceived differences in how ecological emotions—particularly eco-anxiety, eco-grief, and eco-guilt—are expressed or withheld depending on gender. Female participants frequently described being more open and emotionally expressive, citing crying, verbalizing their concerns, or actively discussing environmental issues with peers. They felt it was acceptable—if not expected—for women to express such emotions. As one female participant explained:

“I think those who usually care more are the moms or, generally, girls. As a girl, I tend to worry easily about my surroundings, about nature, and where I am. I feel like girls are more likely to express what they feel. There are men who are expressive too, but I think they’re more likely to keep it to themselves and ask themselves first, ‘Do I really need to share this?” (Participant 9, Female, 21, Rural)

Another participant echoed a similar view, emphasizing how women’s emotionality contributes to their heightened ecological concern:

“Women are more emotional than men, and I think women care more about the environment. Because they’re more emotional, they tend to think more about environmental issues than men. It’s not that men don’t care, but they don’t really think much about those kinds of things. Men are usually more focused on other matters.” (Participant 4, Female, 21, Rural)

Interestingly, male participants shared similar observations. Some noted that although men also feel concern or frustration, they often express it differently—less through overt emotion and more through acknowledgment or discussion in neutral tones. As male participant echoed this, noting:

“I think it’s mostly the females who are more aware and genuinely concerned about these issues—like, they really care. In my experience, women are also more vocal when expressing their emotions. When my female friends talk about these things, I can really feel their anger or frustration. But with my male friends, it’s more like they agree or acknowledge that something is wrong, but it’s rare for me to actually hear or feel any strong emotion from them.” (Participant 6, Male, 22, Urban)

Theme 2: Responsibility Rooted in Nurturing Roles

Several female participants associated their ecological concern with a sense of duty rooted in traditional nurturing roles. They felt accountable not just for themselves, but also for how ecological harm might affect others—particularly family members, future children, or younger generations. This perceived responsibility seemed to deepen their feelings of eco-guilt and ecological worry. As one participant explained:

“Uhm… ah, I think with men it’s more rare. For example, my female friends tend to care more about things like segregating trash in school. Men, on the other hand, often just throw things wherever there’s space—they don’t really think about whether it’s plastic and should go somewhere else.” (Participant 4, Female, 21, Rural)

Beyond immediate caregiving, some women also linked their sense of responsibility to potential future roles as mothers. They reflected on how their ecological awareness shaped the way they thought about childbearing and the world their children might inherit. This anticipation of future responsibility extended their ecological emotions toward imagined generations. As one participant shared:

“I guess being a woman has some factor to it, since as a woman, it’s up to my choice if I choose to have children in the future. I feel like if I decide to have children, I would also have to consider factors like the future environmental state of my future children. I feel like there aren’t a lot of men who would consider those as a factor. They tend to have other priorities—maybe more economic ones. That’s what they would consider rather than these.” (Participant 5, Female, 21, Urban)

This sense of accountability—both present and future—illustrates how ecological emotions among women were shaped by socially reinforced caregiving expectations. For many, nurturing extended beyond people to encompass care for the environment itself, transforming what might otherwise be a personal concern into a moral and relational duty.

Theme 3: Emotional Resonance and Collective Grief

This theme captures how Gen Z participants were not only aware of their own ecological emotions but also deeply affected by the emotional reactions of others—particularly those within their social and peer networks. Unlike Theme 1, which focused on how individuals regulate and express their eco-emotions based on gender norms, this theme explores how emotions are socially transmitted, especially through moments of collective expression and shared distress.

Participants described instances where their friends’ or peers’ emotional responses—especially those of female friends—amplified their own awareness or concern. This phenomenon of “catching” emotions, also known as emotional contagion, shaped their experience of the ecological crisis as a shared emotional burden, not merely a personal one. One male participant reflected on how his emotional landscape was shaped by those around him:

“I think being a woman plays a role here, since I’ve noticed that women tend to be more emotionally expressive. And when I see my female friends talk about these issues, I can really sense how affected they are. I end up feeling it too—sometimes their emotions rub off on me.” (Participant 7, Male, 21, Rural)

Another participant echoed this idea of collective awareness and empathy:

“When they talk about it passionately—especially my girlfriends—you can really feel the sadness or anger. Even if I wasn’t feeling it strongly before, it starts to hit me. It is like a group feeling.” (Participant 4, Female, 21, Rural)

In these narratives, the ecological crisis became more than a personal concern—it became socially lived and emotionally shared. The grief of one participant often evoked a ripple effect, deepening others’ sense of urgency, worry, or sadness. This underscores the role of peer dynamics and group sentiment in shaping emotional responses to environmental degradation.

Rural participants consistently described witnessing environmental degradation firsthand. Rather than viewing the ecological crisis as distant or abstract, they spoke of polluted rivers they used to swim in, deforestation in their hometowns, and agricultural decline experienced by their families. These direct encounters made the threats of climate change feel more personal and urgent.

So, I think my living in a rural place affects it, because there’s a big difference between rural and urban areas. In contrast, urban participants often mentioned daily environmental stressors such as traffic, pollution, flooding, or extreme heat. While these narratives conveyed frustration and concern, they primarily framed environmental decline as part of daily inconvenience or diminished livability. Their concerns reflected exposure to harm, but not necessarily a deeper sense of personal or cultural loss. As one participant explained:

“I feel like… being in an urban area does affect these worries. I often see news about flooding—like, even just a bit of rain and it already floods. When you go to Manila, you really have to be cautious about those things. And it also gets really hot quickly, since there aren’t many trees around. But when you go to rural areas, it feels more breathable—it’s still hot, but there’s more air, you can really feel it. It doesn’t look as nice anymore—it’s mostly infrastructure now.” (Participant 5, Female, 21, Urban)

Theme 5: Disruption of Tradition and Place Identity

Urban participants described the emotional toll of witnessing once-familiar landscapes undergo rapid transformation due to modernization and environmental degradation. These accounts revealed how the ecological crisis intersects with a deeper sense of place-based grief—a sadness that stems not only from environmental harm, but from the loss of memories, routines, and cultural touchstones associated with those places. The narrative tone was one of nostalgia, mourning, and emotional dislocation.

Participants spoke about green spaces, rice fields, and open areas that were once central to their everyday lives, particularly during childhood, but have since been replaced by infrastructure, traffic, and commercial development. These changes appeared to evoke feelings of sadness, disconnection, and a growing sense that the places they once called home no longer reflected their identity or sense of belonging.

“Since new establishments were built, our area became more modernized… the places where we used to play when we were kids—like rice fields—they’re all gone. Now, it’s always traffic. It makes me sad, like the Marikina I experienced when I was younger is no longer there.” (Participant 6, Male, 22, Urban)

“I remember Manila Bay used to be beautiful, but now there are so many buildings… you can see it’s not really that beautiful anymore because it’s all infrastructure now.” (Participant 5, Female, 21, Urban)

In contrast to the rural narratives, which focused on environmental disruption as a threat to survival or livelihood, urban participants often framed their eco-anxiety through the lens of identity loss and emotional detachment. The negative consequences of environmental decline were experienced not only in terms of physical discomfort (e.g., heat, flooding), but also as the erosion of continuity between past and present—of witnessing their city shift away from what it once meant to them.

Discussion

Guided by a mixed-methods sequential explanatory (QUAN → qual) design, the present discussion brings together the quantitative patterns identified in Study 1 and the qualitative narratives explored in Study 2 to illuminate how Generation Z Filipinos experience and make sense of eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief.

Study 1

This study investigated eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief among Gen Z Filipinos, and examined how these ecological emotions vary by gender and place of residence. Our quantitative findings revealed that participants exhibited high levels of habitual ecological worry, eco-guilt, and eco-grief, yet reported relatively low negative functional consequences. Notably, females demonstrated significantly higher habitual ecological worry, eco-guilt, and eco-grief than males, though both genders reported similar levels of functional impairment. Rural residents experienced greater negative consequences from eco-anxiety than urban residents, despite comparable levels of ecological worry, guilt, and grief across residential contexts.

Study 1 findings indicate that Gen Z Filipinos experience elevated levels of affective ecological distress, characterized by high habitual worry, guilt, and grief that exceed normative midpoints. However, the relatively lower scores on the negative consequences subscale suggest that while these youth are deeply preoccupied with the ecological crisis, their distress may manifest more as a chronic internal state than as an acute expectation of immediate personal disaster. This elevated emotional burden was particularly pronounced among female participants, who consistently reported greater worry, guilt, and grief than their male counterparts—a trend that aligns with broader psychological literature on gendered responses to environmental threats. 32

Geographically, while place of residence did not influence general affective worry or grief, the heightened anxiety regarding negative consequences among rural residents suggests that proximity to environmental degradation and climate vulnerability may shape specific dimensions of ecological anxiety. 8 28 Taken together, these results highlight a generation that is emotionally attuned to global ecological decline yet influenced by demographic and locational factors in how they perceive personal risk.

Study 2 qualitative findings expound the nuanced, lived experiences of Gen Z’s eco-emotions, providing a socio-cultural context for the observed gender and residential disparities. Gendered differences are characterized by a higher emotional expressivity among women, who frame their ecological worry through the lens of socially reinforced nurturing roles and a perceived moral duty to future generations 68 8; conversely, men tend to adopt more neutral, detached tones despite sharing similar underlying concerns. This emotional landscape is further intensified by emotional contagion, where collective grief within peer networks transforms personal anxiety into a shared social burden.

Regarding residence, the qualitative data reveals a dichotomy between proximity and identity: rural participants experience eco-anxiety as an urgent, personal threat tied to the tangible degradation of livelihoods and natural resources, 366970 whereas urban participants process environmental decline through place-based grief. For urbanites, the transformation of familiar landscapes into industrialized spaces triggers a sense of nostalgia and “solastalgia,” where the negative consequences of the ecological crisis are felt as an erosion of cultural identity and a loss of connection to their personal history.

Gendered Emotional Expression

Both quantitative results and qualitative accounts indicated that females express ecological emotions more openly than males. Participants perceived women as more proactive and vocal about environmental concerns, while men tended to acknowledge issues in more reserved, neutral tones. These findings align with research demonstrating greater environmental concern and willingness to express such emotions among women.434448 71 However, a critical interpretive question remains about whether these differences reflect genuinely distinct emotional experiences or socialized patterns of emotional expression. Gender role socialization may encourage women to articulate environmental distress while discouraging equivalent expression among men. The finding that both genders reported similar functional consequences suggests that underlying stress responses may be more comparable than overt expression implies.

Responsibility Rooted in Nurturing Roles

Female participants linked their ecological concern to caregiving responsibilities extending to future generations. Several expressed uncertainties about childbearing given environmental degradation, which is consistent with findings from existing research on eco-reproductive concerns.72734574 44 This pattern reflects how the socialization of women into caregiving roles may amplify ecological emotions by expanding the perceived scope of responsibility beyond the self to encompass families, communities, and unborn generations. The extension of nurturing tendencies to encompass nature itself may partially explain the elevated levels of eco-guilt and eco-grief observed among women in this study.

Emotional Resonance and Collective Grief

Participants described how others’ ecological emotions influenced their own, with expressions of grief creating ripple effects that amplified collective concern. This emotional contagion phenomenon 75 76 77 suggests that ecological emotions are not merely individual psychological states but socially transmitted experiences. The collective nature of eco-grief has important implications for both stress propagation and potential intervention: while shared distress may intensify anxiety, it may also foster solidarity and motivate collective climate action. Cunsolo and Landman similarly documented how actively listening to others’ environmental losses can evoke eco-grief even without direct personal experience—a finding our participants corroborated.

Proximity to Environmental Harm

Elevated negative consequences from eco-anxiety among rural dwellers in our study reflect their direct exposure to environmental degradation. Unlike urban residents, whose environmental stressors (heat, pollution, flooding) are often framed as daily inconveniences, rural communities experience ecological change as an existential threat to livelihoods dependent on natural resources.2446576 The qualitative findings provide further context to this pattern, illustrating how rural participants encounter environmental degradation firsthand, making ecological threats more immediate and emotionally impactful. Meanwhile, urban participants tend to frame environmental concerns in relation to daily stressors such as heat, flooding and pollution. This distinction between abstract concern and concrete impact may explain why rural and urban residents report similar worry levels yet differ in functional consequences. The finding aligns with research indicating that proximity to environmental harm intensifies the psychological burden of ecological crisis. However, the findings from the personal experiences of the selected interview participants should be interpreted as context-specific to our sample; as such, the analysis is intended to highlight how proximity to environmental harm is experienced within a particular setting.

Disruption of Tradition, Place Identity Loss, and Emotional Dislocation

Urban participants articulated a distinctive form of ecological grief centered on place-based identity loss. Narratives of childhood landscapes transformed by development evoked nostalgia and disconnection; what Benham and Hoerst 16 78 describe as unsettled place attachment. This place-based grief differs qualitatively from rural concerns about resource depletion; it represents the erosion of continuity between past and present selves. The ‘extinction of experience’ accompanying urbanization may thus generate eco-anxiety rooted in identity disruption rather than immediate survival concerns.

The ecological crisis has profoundly disrupted the traditional Filipino connection to the land, transforming cherished landscapes into sources of deep emotional distress. As these natural areas are replaced by industrialization and modernization, individuals experience an erosion of (a sense of belonging), leading to a “place-based grief” where their home no longer reflects their identity (e.g., ). This “extinction of experience” represents a loss of continuity between their past and present selves, as the environmental stability essential for their psychological well-being is replaced by a lingering ontological insecurity.

Theoretical Integration

Giddens’ 1991 theory of ontological security provides a unifying framework for these findings. Ecological emotions may arise from disruptions to the sense of environmental stability essential for psychological wellbeing. 79 7pagpanumdompanunuluyan The continuous environmental losses documented by our participants, whether degraded rural landscapes or transformed urban spaces, appear to induce ontological insecurity, which manifests as anxiety, guilt, and grief. While the quantitative scales used to measure these emotions were not formally adapted, this mixed-methods approach provides the necessary cultural grounding by using qualitative findings to interpret these standardized constructs. Specifically, the high reliability of the EAQ-22, EGuiQ-11, and EGriQ-6 suggests that these theoretically grounded constructs resonate with the experiences of Gen Z Filipinos. This psychometric evidence, contextualized through indigenous concepts like (remembrance/nostalgia) and (belonging), ensures that the study’s conceptual coherence is rooted in the specific lived realities of the Global South. Critically, this framework suggests that ecological emotions, while distressing, may also motivate adaptive responses as individuals seek to restore security through environmental engagement and community action.

Our findings revealed a dissociation between high ecological worry and low functional impairment warrants further investigation. While this dissociation is noteworthy, our study cannot definitively explain its occurrence. This pattern may reflect effective coping mechanisms, strong social support networks, or cultural factors our participants that buffer against stress-related dysfunction. Alternatively, it may indicate that current measures inadequately capture culturally specific expressions of ecological distress. Understanding why some individuals maintain functioning despite significant ecological concern has implications for identifying protective factors and developing targeted interventions. These cultural explanations are speculative, while our data confirms the “high worry/low impairment” pattern, the reasons (such as cultural resilience) are inferred trends that require further empirical investigation.

Triangulation

By integrating Study 1 and Study 2 we reveal a complex emotional landscape among Gen Z Filipinos, where statistical trends are deeply contextualized by personal and cultural narratives. Quantitatively, Study 1 identifies a unique “dissociation” where our participants report high levels of habitual ecological worry, guilt, and grief that exceed normative midpoints, yet maintain relatively low functional impairment. Qualitative themes from Study 2 explain these disparities through social and geographical lenses: for instance, while Study 1 found that females report significantly higher levels of emotional distress, Study 2 suggests this is driven by Gendered Emotional Expression and Responsibility Rooted in Nurturing Roles, as women often frame ecological concern through a moral duty to future generations.

Geographically, although both groups share similar levels of general worry, rural residents in our sample significantly reported higher negative consequences from eco-anxiety, which the theme Proximity to Environmental Harm attributes to their direct, survival-based exposure to agricultural decline and deforestation. Conversely, urban participants navigate a distinctive place-based grief characterized by pagpanumdombukidpanunuluyan (remembrance/nostalgia) for lost childhood landscapes like (rice fields), experiencing the crisis as an erosion of cultural identity and (belonging) rather than an immediate physical threat.

Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, sample size, as the overrepresentation of females and urban residents in our investigations may bias findings. While the present study aimed to examine gender differences, the disproportionate number of female participants compared to male participants represents a methodological constraint. This imbalance potentially introduces a self-selection bias, as individuals with higher baseline ecological distress—disproportionately women in this sample—may have been more motivated to participate. While the sample size for men remained sufficient for certain comparative analyses, the disparity may have influenced the variance and the overall representativeness of the emotional profiles generated. Consequently, the results should be interpreted with caution when applied to male-identifying individuals. Future research should employ purposive sampling strategies to achieve a more equitable gender distribution and verify if these emotional patterns hold across more balanced cohorts. The Study 2 sample of nine participants, while within qualitative research guidelines, constrained our ability to draw robust comparisons across gender and residential categories. Future studies should ensure balanced representation and larger qualitative samples to enable meaningful subgroup analyses.

Second, voluntary response sampling introduces self-selection bias; individuals with stronger ecological concerns may have been more motivated to participate. Third, the cross-sectional design captures eco-emotions at a single timepoint, precluding examination of how these emotions develop or fluctuate in response to environmental events. Longitudinal designs would better illuminate the temporal dynamics of ecological emotions and their relationship to ongoing crises.

Fourth, the Philippine-specific context limits generalizability. While this specificity is also a strength: contributing Global South perspectives to a predominantly WEIRD literature, future research should extend to other climate-vulnerable nations to identify shared versus culturally distinct patterns. Fifth, our binary treatment of gender excludes LGBTQIA+ perspectives, despite evidence that these populations face heightened climate vulnerability. 80 Future research should incorporate diverse gender identities.

Lastly, our study concerns the use of the original English versions of the instruments without formal cultural adaptation or pilot testing within the local context. While English is the primary medium of instruction and the official language for professional and academic assessments in the Philippines, the absence of a rigorous translation-back-translation process may have overlooked specific cultural nuances or idiomatic expressions unique to the Filipino population. Although the inclusion criterion of a ‘fair understanding of English’ was implemented to ensure comprehension and all quantitative measures show reasonable reliability coefficients in the present study, self-reported proficiency may not fully account for subtle linguistic barriers that could influence item interpretation. Specifically, the strong internal consistency of the EAQ-22, EGuiQ-11, and EGriQ-6 suggests that these theoretically grounded constructs resonate with the experiences of Gen Z Filipinos. This psychometric evidence, combined with the high level of English proficiency among the participants, provides a baseline level of validation that supports the manuscript’s claims regarding the manifestation of ecological emotions in a climate-vulnerable Global South nation. As such, to enhance the psychometric robustness of these findings, future research should prioritize the cultural and linguistic validation of these scales. This includes conducting cognitive interviews and pilot testing to ensure the instruments are culturally resonant. Subsequent studies might also benefit from developing and validating versions in Filipino or other local languages to compare responses across different linguistic demographics within the Philippines, thereby ensuring broader inclusivity and cross-cultural equivalence.

Several promising directions emerge from our findings. The role of socioeconomic status, raised by participants themselves, merits investigation given evidence that lower SES increases vulnerability to environmental harms. 81 Intergenerational comparisons could illuminate whether Gen Z’s ecological emotions reflect developmental stage, historical context, or both. In addition, participants’ accounts also offered preliminary indications that eco-emotions may be experienced and shared within peer networks, suggesting that social network approaches may be useful for further exploring how ecological distress may circulate among individuals and how peer support might be leveraged for intervention. Finally, the apparent disconnect between high worry and low functional impairment invites investigation of protective factors that may potentially inform resilience-focused interventions.

Conclusion and Implications

This study provides preliminary empirical insight into ecological emotions among a climate-vulnerable Global South population, contributing to a growing but still limited body of research in this area. By offering initial evidence from a climate-vulnerable Global South context, these findings underscore the need for expanded empirical investigation and context-sensitive approaches to understanding ecological emotions.

Gen Z Filipinos experience substantial eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief, with important variations by gender and residential context. Females report higher levels of habitual ecological worry, guilt, and grief. These patterns are likely shaped by gendered socialization into caregiving roles and differential norms around emotional expression. Nevertheless, while women may be more emotionally expressive, concerns about the ecological crisis had similarly low levels of impact on psychological functioning across both genders. Rural residents experience greater functional consequences from eco-anxiety, reflecting direct dependence on threatened natural resources. Urban residents, meanwhile, articulate place-based grief tied to the psychological consequences of rapid urbanization, including the loss of green spaces, increased pollution, and the replacement of natural habitats.

Drawing from the theory of Ontological Security or Insecurity, our findings suggest that Gen Z Filipinos’ ecological emotions arise from disruptions in their sense of security within their surroundings. The continuous environmental loss further induces ontological insecurity, which manifests as eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief. However, these same emotions may also motivate individuals to seek other ways of security through environmental care and community engagement.

For mental health practitioners, these findings underscore the importance of recognizing ecological emotions as legitimate psychological experiences warranting clinical attention particularly among young people in climate-vulnerable regions. The low negative psychological consequences of eco-anxiety despite high levels of worry suggest that most Gen Z Filipinos are coping adaptively, but vigilance is warranted for those whose functioning deteriorates. For educators and environmental programs, acknowledging the emotional dimensions of ecological crisis rather than focusing solely on information provision may enhance engagement and wellbeing. Group-based interventions that leverage the collective nature of eco-emotions may be particularly effective.

By situating eco-anxiety, eco-guilt, and eco-grief within socio-cultural and place-based contexts, this study contributes to an emerging literature recognizing that ecological emotions are shaped by culture, community, and geography. Understanding these contextual influences is essential for developing interventions that resonate with the lived experiences of those most affected by environmental crisis.
