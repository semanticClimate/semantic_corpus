A high-resolution boundary current product from Gridded Observations of eXpendable BathyThermograph (XBT) transects

Figures: 10 | Tables: 2 | Source: europe_pmc_PMC13121742.xml

Abstract

The Ship Of Opportunity Expendable BathyThermograph (SOOP-XBT) Network has been measuring upper ocean temperature aboard volunteer vessels over multiple decades in key locations of the global ocean. Through international collaboration, these data are systematically collected, quality-controlled and made available to the scientific and operational communities. The network’s strengths: its low cost, high spatial resolution, and long-term repeatability, have made it a key asset for advancing ocean state estimation and climate studies. Here, we introduce a synthesis of formatted XBT data from selected transects across the global ocean, and provide a high-quality gridded product of temperature and derived salinity and absolute geostrophic velocity across five major boundary currents: the Gulf Stream, Kuroshio, East Australian Current, Brazil Current and the Antarctic Circumpolar Current. The Gridded Observations of XBT transects (GOXBT) product offers a unique temporal coverage and spatial resolution, providing a critical resource for ocean model and reanalysis validation, and supporting future studies on the variability and dynamics of major ocean currents.

Background

The transport and redistribution of mass, heat, salt, and biogeochemical properties within the world’s oceans and the exchanges of these properties between the ocean and atmosphere are largely controlled by ocean boundary currents. The variability of boundary currents has a strong influence on many aspects of weather and climate, for example. Due to their strong temperature gradient and huge amounts of heat and moisture released into the atmosphere123458, boundary currents play a key role in the formation and development of storm tracks. Studies have shown impacts of boundary current strength and location on coastal sea level changes and on the evolution and propagation of marine heatwave events.

The global Ship of Opportunity eXpendable BathyThermographs (SOOP-XBT) Network is a long-standing international effort that collects high-density upper ocean temperature data across fixed repeat transects (Fig. 1910111213151621in-situ). The high spatial resolution along transects enables resolving the sharp temperature gradient across the strong boundary currents, a capability not easily performed by other observing systems. XBT deployments have been collected for more than 50 years, with over 2.35 million profiles in the World Ocean Database dating from the 1960s. One of the main objectives of the SOOP-XBT network is to monitor the variability of global ocean boundary currents and transport at choke points where water masses are exchanged between ocean basins. Because of its high horizontal (25 km or less) and vertical (~0.7 m from surface to 800 m) sampling density, and repeatability (quarterly for two decades or more), the SOOP-XBT transect measurements are well-suited for investigating mesoscale variability (e.g., refs. ) as well as for conducting long-term climate studies (e.g., refs. ). In addition, XBT observations can be complemented by other observational data from and remote sensing platforms, such as Argo floats and satellite altimetry, to give a high-temporal and large-scale view of the processes affecting boundary currents.

Although efforts have been made in the past by several individual institutions to provide curated SOOP-XBT profile data, to date there has been no coordinated initiative to produce a standardized, quality-controlled gridded product readily available for use by the scientific observational and modeling community. This study serves as a foundation for producing an easy-access, well-documented, high-quality XBT gridded product focused on boundary currents. The paper is organized as follows: following this introduction a description of the methods is provided, details on the data collected along each monitoring line are then reported, and a comparison with other gridded products is shown.

Methods

Raw data

XBT probes are equipped with a thermistor that detects changes in electrical resistance when the probe enters the ocean and closes the circuit in contact with seawater222324et al2526272829. A data recording system aboard the vessel converts the resistance and descent time measurements into temperature and depth values. Temperature is calculated using thermistor resistance - temperature equations (R-T equations), while depth is estimated as a function of elapsed time using the empirical fall-rate equation coefficients of Hanawa .. Depending on the type of probe, temperature profiles collected typically extend from near the surface to approximately 800 m depth. Data collectors contributing to repeat transects in the SOOP-XBT network follow quality assurance procedures outlined in ref. , perform automatic quality control (autoQC) on the data. Most profiles are transmitted in near real time to weather bureaus using the Global Transmission System (GTS). Data from the GTS has historically been collected and stored by the Global Temperature and Salinity Profile Programs (GTSPP). In addition to real-time quality-control (QC), SOOP-XBT profiles undergo a delayed-mode QC to provide the best quality data for scientific purposes. The final delayed-mode QC data are available from the repositories in the ‘Data Availability’ section.

From all active transects included in Fig. 111, five were selected in the version 1 of this product. These five transects represent an important subset of the subtropical boundary currents sampled by the XBT network (Fig. ), including the Gulf Stream (GS) and Brazil Current (BC) in the Atlantic, the Kuroshio and East Australian Current (EAC) in the Pacific, as well as the Antarctic Circumpolar Current (ACC) in the Pacific sector of the Southern Ocean. The summary of the five major transects, designated with “AX” for Atlantic and “PX” for Pacific, is shown in Table .

Mapping methodology

Individual XBT profile data was pre-processed by removing all data flagged as ‘suspect’ and ‘bad’, as well as data outside the region of each SOOP transect. A simple mapping methodology was then applied to interpolate the cleaned temperature profiles to a regular grid. Following Goes et al10., we defined a mean track and direction for each transect, either zonal (east-west, longitudinal variation) or meridional (north-south, latitudinal variation). If two profiles were less than 10 km apart, they were averaged into a single profile. Incomplete profiles with missing data were filled using linear interpolation from adjacent profiles. Each section was then linearly interpolated onto the main direction of the nominal transect using a regular grid with 0.1 degrees spacing and a vertical resolution of 2 m. The result of this procedure is a spatially gridded temperature data product for each repeat of the five transects analyzed in this study.

Salinity estimates for temperature profiles

Ocean density is determined by temperature, salinity, and pressure, and it is essential for velocity and transport calculations. Since XBT probes measure only temperature with depth-inferred, several methods have been proposed to determine salinity from available historical data (e.g., refs. 3031et al321). In this study, we used the RSEAS method of Goes . to estimate salinity along the XBT sections. Based on historical simultaneous temperature and salinity profiles (e.g., CTD, Argo), this method uses a local linear regression to determine salinity at defined depths using temperature, temperature squared, and salinity seasonality (annual and semiannual harmonics) as predictors. This method provides global coverage between 75°S and 60°N, and is applicable to the five transects used in this study (Fig. ).

Velocity from temperature and salinity

Large-scale ocean currents (order of 100 km) are primarily driven by two main parameters, water density changes and wind stress, and both are affected by the Earth’s rotation. The balance between the pressure gradient (due to density differences) and the Earth’s rotation (Coriolis force) forms the basis for estimating geostrophic ocean velocities from observations. This is achieved through the thermal wind equation, in which the vertical shear of the horizontal velocity is proportional to the horizontal gradients of dynamic height referenced to a specific reference level. Dynamic height was calculated using temperature and salinity profiles. We chose a zero-velocity reference level near 760 m, corresponding to the manufacturer-defined maximum nominal depth of the most common probe types used in the dataset (T7 and Deep Blue). An exception was made for the BC, which is confined to the top 500 m due to its complex vertical structure, thus 500 m depth was used as the reference level in that case. The absolute geostrophic velocity, independent of the reference level, was then calculated following the methodology of Goes et al5http://apdrc.soest.hawaii.edu33.. For this, a correction was performed to the dynamic height by imposing a mean dynamic height at the chosen reference level using the annual long-term mean absolute dynamic height from the Argo/altimetry blended product developed by the International Pacific Research Center (IPRC), available at (). Incorporating this reference dynamic height also allows a better comparison between the means of dynamic height and satellite sea surface height (SSH), but their variability remains independent of each other. The mean absolute geostrophic velocities across the XBT transects were compared with the those computed using the Roemmich and Gilson Argo climatology from 2004–2023 (herein RG Argo climatology), using only the months that were sampled by the XBTs, thus only summer months for the ACC, even months for the BC, and all months for the other currents.

Volume transport calculation

The evolution, behavior, variability, and strength of a boundary current can be described by its volume transport. In this paper, we estimated the longest available in-situ34 time series of volume transport for major boundary currents, including the GS and BC in the Atlantic, the Kuroshio and EAC in the Pacific, and the ACC in the Southern Ocean. The transport was calculated for a particular current by first defining the current core as the maximum average velocity in the top 50 m, and its boundaries where the velocity reverses direction. Once the current horizontal extent was established, velocities were integrated vertically from the surface to the deepest level of the section, generally to 760 m. Some currents extend deeper in the water column, the ACC for example, and hence these velocity estimates may only represent the upper part of the flow. To validate the transport estimates, we used daily ⅛ degree satellite SSH data from the Copernicus Marine and Environment Monitoring Service (CMEMS; 10.48670/moi-00149). The SSH data was first linearly interpolated to the nominal XBT grid, and the SSH differences across the current boundaries (a proxy for surface velocity) were correlated with the volume transport of the current in the upper 700–800 m.

Data Record

The Gridded Observations of XBT transects (GOXBT) dataset35https://zenodo.org/communities/xbt-dmt/2 presented in this manuscript is publicly available from Zenodo at (10.5281/zenodo.17128134), and no registration is needed to download the dataset. One file is available for each transect (5 total) in netCDF4 format. The files have the name convention GOXBT_”transect”_YYYY0_YYYYE_v1.nc, where “transect” is AX32, AX97, PX30, PX40, PX36, which are available at a spatial resolution of 0.1 deg along the main axis of the transect, from year YYYY0 to year YYYYE, and conformed with version 1 (“v1”) of this product. As a netCDF file, the data can be easily visualized in any common software used in oceanographic research (Python, MATLAB, NCL, etc.).

Technical Validation

Main characteristics

In this section, to validate the robustness of the gridded XBT dataset, we examine the sampling strategy and main physical characteristics (temperature, salinity and absolute geostrophic velocity) of each of the five main current systems.

AX32: Gulf Stream (2009–2025)

XBT profiles have been collected from CMV Oleander2a12b2d along transect AX32, which spans from New York/Jersey to Bermuda, for nearly 50 years. In the early years, beginning in 1977, temperature data were collected only shoreward of the GS north wall (see Data Availability section). In 2009, the temperature section was extended to reach Bermuda, with the goal of conducting 12 cruises per year and deploying 48 probes per cruise (Fig. ; Table ). A total of 102 realizations that resolve the GS were collected from July 2009 to March 2025, with sampling fairly evenly distributed across all months (Fig. ). The spatial resolution of probe deployments ranges from approximately 15 km within the Gulf Stream to 25–50 km in the ocean interior (Fig. ).

The product shows a strong gradient in the mean temperature and salinity along transect AX32 near 38°N (Fig. 2d,e36382f). This sharp gradient marks the GS north wall, where cooler and fresher subpolar waters on the inshore side contrast with warmer, saltier subtropical waters offshore. South of the front, a thermostad, a thick layer of nearly uniform temperature, is evident. This feature corresponds to the Subtropical Mode Water, commonly referred to as “18-Degree Water”, which forms just south of the GS. The dominant oceanic feature across the section is the GS current, which can be derived geostrophically from the high-resolution XBT gridded product. Using this method, the GS core is typically located near 38°N, with velocities between 0.6 and 0.8 m/s (Fig. ). In this region, the GS has separated from the coast and flows as a free jet, making it more susceptible to intensified mesoscale eddy activity. As a result, the GS path exhibits high variability, and the current observed in individual cruises is often stronger than the long-term mean.

In addition to deploying XBTs, the CMV Oleander223940 is also equipped with instruments for surface Ocean CO measurements, a Thermosalinograph, and a Continuous Plankton Recorder, being able to monitor fronts in temperature, salinity and CO concentrations aligned with the (sub)mesoscale circulation patterns. These sustained multi-parameter observations have revealed significant long-term changes in the region, including the warming and shrinking of the Slope Sea, a northward shift of the GS, and the warming of the 18 Degree Water to 19 °C.

AX97: Brazil Current (2004–2024)

Since August 2004, upper ocean temperature profiles have been collected along transect AX97, which spans from Rio de Janeiro (22.9°S, 43°W) to Trindade Island (20°S, 30°W) (Fig. 3a3b153d). This effort is part of a long-standing collaboration between Brazilian universities, the Brazilian Navy, the Brazilian Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA). The transect is occupied bimonthly, typically during even-numbered months (Fig. ), aligned with the Brazilian Navy’s resupply missions to Trindade Island. As the longest continuously operating monitoring system of the BC to date, AX97 has accumulated 21 years of observational data. Between August 2004 and June 2025, a total of 106 cruises were conducted, deploying 5,380 XBTs to measure the upper ocean thermal structure down to approximately 800 m. This transect intersects the BC near 22.8°S and the 200 m isobath, a dynamic region marked by intensified mesoscale activity, including cyclonic meanders and frequent eddy formation. Sampling resolution ranges from roughly 18 km near the continental shelf break to about 27 km in offshore areas (Fig. ).

The mean temperature and salinity characteristics along the AX97 transect exhibit a pronounced thermohaline gradient between longitudes 41°W and 39°W (Fig. 3d,e3f). This gradient delineates the crossing of the BC, characterized by the southward transport of warmer and saltier tropical waters. The geostrophic velocities of the BC reveal an average southward flow of approximately 0.2 m/s (Fig. ). The BC extends from the surface down to depths of 400–500 m, with its core centered between 50 and 100 m. Below this level, a positive velocity signal is observed, indicating the upper boundary of the northward Intermediate Western Boundary Current (IWBC). This structure reflects the baroclinic nature of the BC–IWBC system, characterized by opposing flows at different depths along the western boundary.

The AX97 data has been used to evaluate how the BC is represented in ocean forecasting systems41542. It has also provided insight into the mean, seasonality, and behavior during extreme events such as its rapid increase during a marine heatwave in 2010. More recently, combined with Argo data, it was used to monitor for the first time the basinwide integrated Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) and meridional heat transport across 22.5°S.

PX30: East Australian Current (1991–2025)

The PX30 XBT line operates between Brisbane, Australia, and Fiji. The PX30 line also forms the northern arm of the ‘Tasman Box’ which includes a southern line, PX34 (Sydney to Wellington, New Zealand) and an eastern transect (PX06, Auckland to Fiji). The aim of the Tasman Box is to explore the variability of mass and heat budgets of this regional western boundary current region43444545444647. Both the PX30 and PX34 lines have provided long-term and continuous monitoring of the EAC, which is an energetic western boundary current with a predominantly southward flow, transporting heat to the South Pacific Ocean. The PX30 line is situated south of the main westward inflows feeding into the boundary flow, and crosses the EAC at about 27°S, where the current is most stable and generally connected to the coast. In contrast, the PX34 line is located in a highly variable region that experiences partial current separation. Previous observations of the EAC have been based on broad-scale measurements from research and merchant ships. Observations from the PX30 line have also provided the long-term context to the higher temporal resolution of a full-depth mooring array which was sited adjacent to the PX30 line.

The PX30 line has been operated by CSIRO since 1991, CSIRO and the Integrated Marine Observing System (IMOS) from 2006, and ongoing support from Scripps Institution of Oceanography and Australian Defence, using 33 different merchant vessels as ships of opportunity. PX30 either passes just south of New Caledonia or more recently, has sometimes been split into two sections with a break in Noumea and in some cases only one of these halves was completed. Ship tracks have varied due to adverse sea conditions or whether the endpoint is either Nadi or Suva in Fiji (Fig. 4a4c4(b,c)). The goal for the PX30 section has been four repeats per year, with an observation approximately every 25 km and more intense sampling in the EAC (approximately every 10 km). Only relatively coarse sampling was performed during the pandemic (e.g., 2019–2021; Fig. ). Over the 34 years of operation, the data collection has been reasonably evenly distributed over the year (Fig. ), approximately 10 repeats for each month, with the least number of transects (5) for January.

The southward jet of the EAC and a tight northward retroflection are the most prominent features in Fig. 4d-f47−1−1−1−14748. The EAC projects eastwards by more than 200 km to about 155.1°E. Individual transects show that the EAC has strong mesoscale activity and its offshore edge is often difficult to distinguish from the many eddies and meanders. The southward flow extends vertically beyond the depth reached by the XBTs (to at least 1500 m), with an intense core in the upper 400 m (>0.2 ms), >0.4 ms in the upper 200 m and 0.67 ms at the surface. At the offshore edge of the mean core the zero contour drops almost vertically through the water column. There is also a distinct northward undercurrent on the continental slope, below 400 m (mean current maximum = 0.045 ms) and extending about 40 km offshore. This is a very persistent feature and is evident in nearly every individual XBT transect. These results closely match the mean patterns obtained from a mooring array which has greater temporal resolution but less spatial coverage of the EAC.

PX36: Antarctic Circumpolar Current (1994–2025; austral summer only)

In 1994, the PX36 XBT monitoring line was established with the aim of monitoring and studying the variability of the ACC in the framework of the Italian National Antarctic Programme (Programma Nazionale di Ricerche in Antartide - PNRA). The PX36 transect extends from the southern coast of Aotearoa New Zealand (Christchurch) to the Ross Sea (Fig. 5a5b495cet al50135113521953). XBT profiles have been collected almost every austral summer, primarily in January and February (Fig. ), aboard the R/V Italica and subsequently aboard the R/V Laura Bassi during 5 to 7 days of navigation. Surface temperature and salinity data were also acquired from the thermosalinograph during the transect and used for remote sensed salinity observations. The mean spatial resolution of the XBT observations ranges between 28 and 37 km. Throughout 29 oceanographic cruises, an average of 116 XBT probes were deployed per cruise, resulting in a total of 3,388 profiles (Fig. ). A general description of the collected data has been recently provided by Aulicino . along with the links to the full XBT dataset. Previous studies showed the contribution of PX36 data to realize a detailed description of the ACC, including the variability of its fronts, the presence of cold and warm core eddies, the long term variability of the ACC flow in the Pacific sector as well as its trends in temperature and the mixed-layer seasonal and interannual variations in the Southern Ocean.

The mean temperature section (Fig. 5d5e13) shows a progressive cooling of the water column from lower to higher latitudes, with sharp horizontal gradients at specific latitudes (i.e., around 54°S, 59–60°S, 62°S and 64°S). These gradients correspond to the mean positions of the Northern Sub-Antarctic Front (NSAF), the Southern Sub-Antarctic Front (SSAF), the Polar Front (PF), and the Southern ACC front (sACCf), which represent the main fronts and jets of the ACC. Additionally, in the southern part of the section a distinct tongue of cold surface water is evident, associated with the presence of Antarctic Surface Water (AASW). This water mass, shaped by atmospheric cooling and sea ice melt, is characterized by relatively low salinity compared with underlying layers (Fig. ). Temperature sections along this monitoring line also present the fingerprint of smaller scale structures, such as meanders, eddies and filaments of the ACC that are also responsible for the meridional transport of water masses, heat, salt and nutrients in the area.

The salinity section (Fig. 5e5455) reveals a different latitudinal pattern than temperature. Salinity decreases southward up to approximately 54°S, where the Subantarctic Front (SAF) is located. In this region, the Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW) forms and is recognisable by its characteristic salinity minimum, which is observed throughout the Southern Ocean. At more southerly latitudes, a near-surface salinity minimum is also observed, associated with sea-ice melt. At greater depths, a salinity maximum is identified, linked to the upwelling of Circumpolar Deep Water (CDW), the major source of heat and nutrients in the Southern Ocean. The elevated salinity signal of the CDW is due in part to the contribution of North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW), which is notably more saline than other deep water masses, partly due to its connection with the saline outflow from the Mediterranean Sea.

The mean zonal velocity along the transect (Fig. 5f) confirms the expected presence of the strong zonal eastward flow of the ACC. The velocity field is characterized by localized maxima associated with the individual fronts and jets. Negative values observed along the section reflect variability in the latitudinal positions of the fronts, particularly the SAF branches and the PF, which in turn affect the position of the dynamic height gradients used in the mean velocity calculation.

PX40: Kuroshio (2012–2025)

The PX40 XBT transect, between Honolulu, USA and Yokohama, Japan, samples the Kuroshio, the western boundary current of the North Pacific Gyre. The transect was initiated in October 1998 by Tohoku University using a Japanese training vessel Miyagi Maru6c6b6c6d with cruises three times per year (March, June and October) and broadscale horizontal sampling of ~50 km. Japanese funding ceased in 2012, and PX40 became part of the Scripps Institution of Oceanography high-resolution XBT network. Since 2012, PX40 has been sampled roughly 4 times a year (Fig. ) with 46 transects to date (2025) that have been roughly equally distributed seasonally (Fig. ). Approximately 190 XBT probes are dropped between Honolulu and Yokohama (Fig. ), every 25–50 km in the interior with a finer resolution of 10 km in the Kuroshio and Kuroshio Extension (KE), where the current is stronger and mesoscale variability is enhanced (Fig. ).

The mean temperature (Fig. 6d6e6f178) and salinity (Fig. ) show the geostrophic signature of the Kuroshio against the coast, with a relatively narrow core (140°E–141.5°E) of strong northward flow of warm water (Fig. ). The Kuroshio crossing PX40 has a time mean of ~70 + /− 8 Sv. A significant decreasing trend in Kuroshio transport across PX40 of −1.2 + /− 0.36 Sv/year was found to have contributions from both a weakening core depth-integrated velocity and a narrowing of the offshore edge. A warming trend in the Kuroshio core of 0.05°–0.1 °C has recently been observed along PX40.

Offshore of the Kuroshio jet, the strong meandering and eddying of the KE is evident in all three properties (Fig. 6d–f15657596017). Using PX40 XBT data combined with XBT data from the PX37 transect (San Francisco-Honolulu; Fig. ), eddies were found to make a substantial but not dominant contribution, to meridional heat transport in the North Pacific, that were mainly due to large-scale exchanges in the subtropical gyre. On decadal time scales, the poleward-flowing Kuroshio-KE system at PX40 was found to have similar pathways and magnitudes during both stable and unstable states of the current in response to the North Pacific wind stress curl, although further offshore the KE meandering differed between the states.

Comparison with other gridded products

GOXBT and Argo RG salinity validation

The RSEAS method has been evaluated in several studies. Salinity uncertainties of this method are generally below 0.23132et al577a–e7b7f2e7a7d, which typically corresponds to errors of ~1 cm in dynamic height, translating to 0.06 m/s in velocity at midlatitudes (See Figure S1 in Goes .). Larger uncertainties may occur near the surface, particularly in frontal regions and areas affected by freshwater runoff. As a controlled evaluation of this method (accounting for seasonality but not temperature differences), we interpolate the RG Argo monthly temperature from January through December of 2024 onto the locations of the reference transects to estimate the RSEAS salinity. The RSEAS estimates are then compared with the corresponding RG Argo salinity profiles for the same months (Fig. ). For better profile visualization (Fig. ), we use every other profile within 2 degrees of the coast (10 profiles), and linearly subsample additional 20 profiles from that point to the end of the section, totaling approximately 30 profiles × 12 months = 360 profiles. RSEAS-derived salinity compares well with RG Argo salinity for all transects, capturing the main vertical structure and seasonal variability. A tight salinity relationship is observed for the AX97 (Fig. ), having generally the lowest root-mean-squared error (RMS < 0.02 below 200 m; Fig. ). The AX32 salinity comparison shows higher differences in the upper 500 m, where the RSEAS captures the near surface freshening near the coast that is not represented by Argo (Fig. ). A tighter relationship along AX32 is observed further offshore, as seen by the grouping of the profiles in the right side of Fig. . The RMS difference is ~0.2 near the surface and 0.07 throughout the 800 m water column. PX40 also has higher differences near the surface with an RMS ~0.17, but this relationship improves with depth. PX36, although located near Antarctica where there is seasonal freshwater input, the two products agree well even for salinities below 33.5 (Fig. ), and RMS < 0.07. In general, this evaluation indicates that the RSEAS method provides accurate salinity estimates, with RMS < 0.2 as shown in previous studies.

GOXBT and ARGO RG Velocity

In this section, the XBT gridded dataset is compared with other gridded products. First, we compare the mean velocity structure from the XBT gridded product with that from the RG Argo climatology (Fig. 88a,e8b,c61428d8d10) using the same methodology. The high resolution of the XBT gridded product shows stronger and sharper boundary currents than RG Argo for all transects. The western boundary currents in the North Atlantic and North Pacific (Fig. ) reach speeds of 0.8 m/s at their core in the XBT gridded product, whereas they only reach up to 0.4 m/s and are more vertically coherent in RG Argo. In the South Atlantic and South Pacific (Fig. ), boundary currents show increased stratification with depth, and intermediate currents counteracting the southern flows below 400 m, consistent with the poleward displacement of the subtropical gyre with depth described by Rhines and Young. Apart from that, these two currents show a clear distinction in terms of their strength, with the EAC reaching speeds of 0.7 m/s, whereas the BC only reaches 0.2 m/s. This is mostly due to the northward contribution of the AMOC to the flow in the South Atlantic, as well as the narrower width of the Atlantic basin, where the wind stress curl integrated across the basin contributes more to the western boundary current transport. For the ACC (Fig. ), results from the gridded PX36 show the jets reaching 0.2 m/s on average, representing the narrow frontal gradients of the ACC that lower resolution products cannot capture (Fig.  right panel). It is important to emphasize that these differences between products are largely due to higher XBT transect resolution, but also to variable data availability in the Argo product, which is crucial for a robust monitoring of the variability of these currents. Accordingly, the weaker correlation observed between XBT-derived and Argo-derived transports in the ACC can be attributed to the fine-scale structure of the ACC jets, which are poorly resolved by Argo floats, and to the sparse float coverage in the study region, especially in the southern segment of PX36.

GOXBT dynamic height and CMEMS altimetry SSH

Using a reference mean to compute absolute dynamic height improves the agreement with altimetry SSH (Fig. 9). In addition to the along-transect mean, the evolution of the surface dynamic height closely tracks the altimetric SSH. Mesoscale activity can be observed in both products, and is especially evident in PX30 in the EAC current system. Altimetric SSH includes contributions from deep (below 800 m) and non-steric processes, and hence long-term trends in SSH and dynamic height are not always comparable. For example, along the AX97, positive SSH positive trends are much stronger than those in dynamic height.

GOXBT and CMEMS altimetry transport estimates

To assess the variability of boundary currents with an independent dataset, the GOXBT estimates of volume transport in the upper 700–800 m are compared with those determined from the altimetric SSH gradient (DSSH), which is a proxy for absolute surface geostrophic velocity captured by satellite altimetry (Fig. 10176210a,e10d10b,c45). Overall, the correlation between the two time series is high, above 0.7 for all transects, showing that the XBT data product successfully represents mesoscale-to-climate variability. The highest correlation value (0.93) is observed for the ACC. This may be attributed to the current’s barotropic structure, whereby the volume transport is more strongly associated with its surface velocity. Such agreement also shows that the gridded XBT product has a great potential to be used with altimetry data to enhance its temporal coverage, a synergy that has been shown in many previous studies between these two datasets (e.g., ref. ), and has been used to generate improved estimates of the transport mean and variability of the currents (e.g., ref. ). In agreement to that shown for the velocity sections, the transport is strongest in the northern boundary currents, GS with ~70 Sv and the Kuroshio with nearly 50 Sv on average (Fig. , respectively). In addition, the ACC can reach values of 100 Sv and averages ~70 Sv across the whole transect (Fig. ). The southern subtropical boundary currents show the weakest mean transports of nearly 5 Sv for the BC and 10.7 Sv for the EAC (Fig. ). The EAC time series of volume transport illustrates a high degree of variability of all the western boundary currents, with southward transport varying from peaks of 34 Sv (February, 1993) to values close to zero (2004, 2008, 2021). This volatility of the flow is associated both with the presence of eddies and with the location of the separation of the current from the coast. These large fluctuations also illustrate the difficulty of obtaining robust estimates of the current strength with sampling limited to 4 transects per year.

Summary of technical validation and potential applications

This paper presents the first community effort to develop a gridded XBT-based product for use in ocean model validation and for studies of global ocean boundary currents. A standardized methodology is described for the gridding of the high-density transect data, salinity estimation, and absolute geostrophic velocity calculation. From a plethora of available transects across all ocean basins, five transects were selected to represent the main western boundary currents in the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, as well as the ACC around the Antarctic continent, which enables monitoring of important choke points in the global oceans. In comparison to low-resolution Argo products, the GOXBT product can better represent the structure of the currents, and its variability is in good agreement with high-resolution satellite altimetry. The new data product has a great potential for validation of climate models and reanalysis data and the study of variability of these currents from mesoscale to climate timescales. In the future, additional currents will be added from other transects (e.g., equatorial, eastern boundary, Indian Ocean, Mediterranean Sea) to further increase representativeness of the global ocean currents data product. Finally, the GOXBT product can be used with other observational estimates to increase coverage in time and depth, as well as explore large scale features such as the AMOC.
