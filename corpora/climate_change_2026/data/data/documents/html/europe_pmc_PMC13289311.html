Transforming climate change and health practice: a follow-up survey of participants in a global online professional education programme

Figures: 1 | Tables: 3 | Source: europe_pmc_PMC13289311.xml

Abstract

Introduction

Climate change poses unprecedented threats to global health, yet formal educational programmes preparing professionals to address this intersection remain limited. The Yale Climate Change and Health Certificate Program, launched in 2018, represents a pioneering online professional education initiative designed to build cross-sectoral capacity in climate and health. This study evaluated long-term outcomes for members of the programme’s first nine cohorts (2018–2023).

Methods

We conducted a mixed-methods evaluation that followed a slightly modified Kirkpatrick’s four-level training evaluation model. Participants responded to questions that assessed Learning and Self-Efficacy through knowledge and confidence measures, Behaviour through career and engagement indicators and Results through work involvement comparisons. Latent class analysis identified distinct behaviour change patterns, while qualitative analysis explored behaviour change pathways through rapid-qualitative assessment. Data were collected via a Qualtrics survey distributed by email.

Results

There were 205 survey respondents. The data demonstrated a gap between learning (ie, knowledge acquisition) and self-efficacy in applied skills: >90% of participants agreed or strongly agreed they understood 4 of 6 areas of knowledge queried; only 70%–78% agreed or strongly agreed they had confidence in applying 3 of 4 skills queried. The programme outcomes were substantial: 91.1% modified their work approach, 76.5% pursued climate activities outside work, 58.7% made career changes and 46.5% pursued additional education. Results showed professional engagement with climate and health increased from 45.4% to 65.9% after programme completion. There were two distinct behaviour change pathways: ‘Integrators’ (50.2%) who embedded climate perspectives into existing roles and ‘Pivoters’ (41.0%) who made substantial career transitions.

Conclusions

The programme achieved learning and self-efficacy, behavioural and impact outcomes across Kirkpatrick levels. The identification of two behaviour change pathways provides a novel framework for understanding professional development impact. The knowledge–application gap highlights the need for enhanced experiential learning components in online professional education. These findings offer evidence-based principles for designing effective climate and health programmes as the field expands.

Keywords: Education, Public Health Practice, Program Evaluation

Introduction

Climate change is arguably the greatest public health challenge of the 21st century, as people globally are facing record-breaking threats to their well-being, health and survival.112456 Recent data shows that 10 of the 15 key indicators for monitoring climate-related health hazards have hit alarming new levels, highlighting the need for urgent action. In response, educational institutions have begun developing training programmes that explore the intersection between climate change and human health., These programmes typically take the form of curricular enhancements within established academic pathways—for instance, weaving climate health content into medical and nursing education or creating specialised concentrations within Master of Public Health (MPH) programmes. Despite increasing awareness of these challenges, formal educational programmes focused on providing professionals with the knowledge and skills to address the intersection of climate and health remain limited. Many healthcare practitioners cite the lack of specialised training as a barrier to deeper engagement in this field and have called for expanded educational pathways.

In 2018, the Yale Center on Climate Change and Health responded to this need by launching the first-of-its-kind, online Climate Change and Health Certificate programme to equip cross-sectoral leadership with the latest climate science and best strategies for strengthening the public health response to climate change. This programme, consisting of video lectures, real-time discussions and weekly assignments, detailed elsewhere, covers three broad areas: (1) an overview of climate change and health, (2) climate change adaptation for health and (3) climate change and health communication. After Cohorts 1–7 were exposed to the initial content during 2018–2022, video lectures were updated and rerecorded to incorporate the latest developments in the field. Furthermore, in response to a past evaluation, starting with Cohort 9 in 2023, the programme was extended from 18 to 21 weeks through addition of three simultaneously taught 3-week regional modules, led by regional experts, where learners could focus on sub-Saharan Africa, the Caribbean or South Asia, respectively.676 The place-based education approach of the modules was designed to enhance applied learning by engaging participants with locally relevant case studies, data and policy contexts, with the goal of enabling more direct translation of knowledge and research into practice. By grounding the content in region-specific contexts, we sought to help learners increase their confidence in applying climate and health competencies within their own settings. Findings from our previous evaluation, using contemporaneous course evaluations and administrative data for 579 enrolees (Cohorts 1–7), included high programme completion, high curricular satisfaction and substantial peer-to-peer learning. Among the limitations of our past evaluation was a focus on initial programme satisfaction, with no follow-up data after programme completion.

To comprehensively evaluate this programme beyond satisfaction, we conducted a survey of programme completers and evaluated the results using a small modification of the Kirkpatrick Model of Training Evaluation, a framework applied in diverse global settings, including evaluations of the effectiveness of health information management programmes in the USA and rural training programmes in India.81011 12, This model is focused on professional education and operationalises training evaluation into four levels: (1) Reaction, referring to short term feelings and takeaways; (2) Learning, relating more directly to acquired knowledge, skills and attitudes; (3) Behaviour, relating to differences in actions attributed to the training and (4) Results, which refers to the macrolevel organisational goals that would define ultimate success. We diverge slightly from Kirkpatrick’s model by explicitly making a distinction between learning (ie, knowledge acquisition) and self-efficacy in Level 2 (now called ‘Learning and Self-efficacy’). We make this distinction because self-efficacy is people’s judgement of their ability to execute a specific course of action and has previously been proven to be a stronger predictor of behaviour than knowledge itself.

Our past evaluation used extant course evaluations, thus focused primarily on a Level 1 evaluation.6 The objective of the current study is to conduct a complete evaluation, integrating Levels 2–4. We report on the findings from our survey of certificate programme completers using a mixed-methods approach to evaluate the programme’s impact on the first nine programme cohorts (2018–2023), collected 1 year or more after certificate completion. The goal of this study is to assess the degree to which the programme has catalysed career, educational, civic and personal engagement at the intersection of climate change and health and enhanced long-term climate-health self-efficacy.

Methods

Study population

Eligible participants in this study included completers of the Climate Change and Health Certificate from the first nine cohorts of the programme, which was offered approximately two times a year between 2018 and 2023.

Data collection

Survey design

We developed a survey (online supplemental appendix A) based on our prior study and current research objectives. The survey instrument was grounded in Kirkpatrick’s four-level training evaluation model, with items designed to map directly to Levels 2–4. Learning and self-efficacy items were adapted from the programme’s stated learning objectives, behaviour items assessed programme-specific career and engagement changes, and results items measured professional involvement in climate and health before versus after programme completion.

The survey included both Likert-scale and free-response questions and aimed to evaluate participants’ current professional roles, their assessment of their learning (ie, knowledge acquisition) and self-efficacy in climate change and health topics and skills, and their assessment of the impact of the certificate programme on their subsequent professional and personal development, as manifested by changes in behaviour (professional, educational and personal trajectories) and results, as demonstrated by professional engagement with climate and health before versus after the programme.

The survey was developed specifically for this programme evaluation rather than as a generalisable psychometric instrument, and formal validation and pilot testing were not conducted. The mixed-methods approach, combining standardised Likert-scale measures with open-ended qualitative responses, allows for triangulation and contextual understanding of participant experiences across data sources.

Survey distribution

We administered the survey via Qualtrics. It was distributed to all completers of the first nine cohorts of the Climate Change and Health Certificate programme using the programme’s email listserv. The data collection period remained open for 6 weeks, with two reminder emails, to allow sufficient time for responses. All participants were required to provide informed consent to participate by selecting ‘yes’ on the consent form prior to accessing the survey (online supplemental appendix B). Respondents had the option to provide their name and email for future correspondence at the end of the survey. These variables, which were securely stored on Qualtrics and were the only personal identifiers collected, were not included in our analytical dataset, such that no personal identifiers were used in the data analysis. The median survey completion time was 6.6 min.

Data analysis

Quantitative analysis

We used descriptive statistics to summarise respondent characteristics and response rates. We then calculated frequencies and percentages for Likert-scale responses to quantify respondents’ self-assessments and perceptions. Afterwards, we conducted a latent class analysis of programme impact and career trajectory questions (see questions 3–9 in online supplemental appendix A13online supplemental table 1). We varied class sizes from 2 to 6 and chose the model that minimised the Akaike Information Criterion and Bayesian Information Criterion and maximised entropy. Interpretable classes were then compared based on participants’ baseline characteristics and self-efficacy responses using t-tests and multivariate analysis of variance. All quantitative analyses were conducted using R V.4.5.0 and latent class analysis was conducted using the poLCA package. Missing data were handled as follows: for Likert-scale items, we calculated percentages based on valid responses, excluding non-responses. For the latent class analysis, non-responses were coded as 6 to distinguish them from ‘No’ (1) and ‘Yes’ (2) responses. We examined missingness patterns for each latent class; those with substantial missingness were excluded from interpretive analysis. Response missingness can be found in .

Qualitative evaluation

We analysed the free-text data using a rapid qualitative analysis method, incorporating multiple iterative rounds of review.14 Two analysts independently reviewed the open-ended responses and developed a preliminary coding framework, organising responses into two overarching categories aligned with the groups described in the latent class analysis. Within each category, analysts then met to compare coding decisions, discuss discrepancies and refine both the categories and subcodes through consensus. Based on these conversations, we refined categories and subcodes and applied them in another round of categorisation. This process was conducted multiple times to ensure consistency and depth of analysis. Finally, we identified exemplary quotes that illustrated the key themes while maintaining participant confidentiality; we documented the frequency of each code to highlight prevalent topics.

Synthesis by Kirkpatrick model levels

We organised our analyses according to Kirkpatrick’s four levels of training evaluation.7table 1 As Level 1 (Reaction) was addressed in our previous publication, this study focused on Levels 2–4. We mapped survey questions to these levels as follows: learning and self-efficacy items assessed Level 2 (Learning and Self-efficacy), questions about the impact of the programme on professional and personal development evaluated Level 3 (Behaviour), and questions about climate work involvement before versus after programme completion measured Level 4 (Results) (). This framework guided both our quantitative analyses and qualitative coding.

Results

Participant characteristics

A total of 205 participants completed the survey (32% response rate; table 2). Survey participants were representative of all certificate completers with respect to region of residence and country income level. Among respondents, most reported residing in North America (73.2%), with smaller proportions from Africa (3.9%) and Europe (3.9%) and less than 7% from South/Central America, Oceania and Asia combined. Approximately 80% reported living in a high-income country; 4.4% reported living in a lower-middle income country; 2.4% reported living in an upper-middle income country; and 1.0% reported living in a low-income country. We note there was a high non-response rate (12.7%) for country of residence (and therefore country income level) for survey respondents. Reported sectors of employment included 24.4% in academia; 11.7% in the business sector; 12.7% in government; 21.5% in healthcare; and 19.0% in the non-profit/non-governmental organisation (NP/NGO) sector. Notable differences between survey participants and completers included more survey participants working in business, government and NP/NGOs and fewer participants working in healthcare and ‘other’. If completion of the certificate programme prompted career changes, one would not expect participants to be representative of all survey completers (from whom data were collected at the time of enrolment into the certificate programme) with respect to industry.

Kirkpatrick evaluation

Level 2: learning and self-efficacy

Learning assessments across six areas and self-efficacy assessments across four areas revealed strong knowledge and confidence in specific topics and skills (figure 1). Participants reported the highest knowledge/understanding of fundamental climate change and health concepts: 94.4% agreed or strongly agreed they understood how climate change impacts vulnerable populations; 94.5% understood the relationship between climate change and health; 93.4% understood climate change mitigation health cobenefits; 91.2% understood the fundamentals of climate change science; 88.4% understood the drivers of climate change beliefs and behaviours; and 72.4% understood the elements of a programme evaluation strategy. Reported self-efficacy measures, while still high, were notably lower than learning measures: 87.8% reported agreeing or strongly agreeing that they feel confident in their abilities to talk about climate change with most people; 78.2% were confident in identifying community, organisational or governmental adaptation opportunities; 76.8% reported confidence in designing a climate change communication campaign; and 70.1% were confident in their ability to design an adaptation plan.

Level 3: behaviour

Most participants (91.1%) reported that completing the programme motivated or enabled them to change the way they engage in their work (table 3). Additionally, 58.7% of respondents reported being motivated to make career changes because of the programme, while 46.5% were motivated to pursue additional degrees or training, and 76.5% were motivated to pursue climate change and health activities outside of their formal work responsibilities.

We conducted a latent class analysis of the questions relating to how the programme inspired personal and career motivation changes to identify if there were patterns of behaviour change. We chose a three-class model to minimise fit statistics and maximise interpretability. We nominally defined Class #1 as the ‘Integrators’ (n=103); that is, those who applied climate and health concepts gained from the certificate programme within their existing professional or academic roles. Class #2 (n=18) was characterised by substantial missingness of responses. Compared with the other classes, the Class #2 respondents had a slightly higher representation from the healthcare and NP sectors, lower representation from academic and business, and the same representation on prior climate work. The small number in this class limits statistical comparison with the other classes (online supplemental tables 2,3online supplemental figure 1online supplemental table 3online supplemental table 3online supplemental tables 4,5). Due to the small number, we did not interpret Class #2 further. We defined Class #3 as the ‘Pivoters’ (n=84), describing those who made a substantial shift across their career, academic or personal trajectories toward climate and health. The probability of responding ‘yes’ to behaviour change questions () demonstrated that Question 6 (‘Has completing the Yale Climate Change and Health Certificate Program motivated you to pursue an additional degree or training?’) most distinguishes Integrators (Class #1) from Pivoters (Class #3). We explored whether quantitative data were otherwise distinguishable between these classes; we did not observe any difference in sector of employment (), prior climate work () or responses to learning and self-efficacy questions ().

Level 4: results

The survey revealed substantial shifts in participants’ professional engagement with climate change and health after the certificate programme (table 3). Before completing the certificate programme, 45.4% of respondents reported their work involved climate change and health, which increased to 65.9% after programme completion, representing a 20.5 percentage point increase in professional engagement with climate change and health.

Qualitative analysis

We explored free-text data responses to understand differences between Integrators and Pivoters. The following sections illustrate these themes through participants’ own words.

Integrators

Participants integrate climate into their work or lead initiatives

Some participants reported actively integrating climate and health perspectives into their work, whether as healthcare professionals engaging with their patients, health departments shaping public initiatives or businesses working with clients. One participant explained, “”“”My work doesn’t directly focus on climate change and health but has impacted the population we work with. It has helped me center my work more and adjust programming to understand how the population we work with is and will be affected by climate change and health outcomes. Others noted that, whether or not their primary roles focus on climate change and health topics, they have taken the initiative to start interest groups or initiatives at their workplace to build awareness. One participant described, I created a group within my company so people could gather to learn about the basic concepts. Share information. And together identify key issues at our local level of our ways of working and consuming and identify potential actions we could implement.

Participants link their academic research to climate

Multiple participants described leveraging the knowledge gained from the certificate programme in their roles as academic or professional researchers. One participant reflected, “”“”Studying climate change and health has significantly influenced and broadened my approach to researching infectious diseases. By understanding how climate factors impact disease dynamics and transmission patterns, I’ve been able to integrate climate change considerations into my malaria research. This interdisciplinary perspective has enriched my work, allowing me to explore novel insights and potential interventions at the nexus of climate, health, and infectious diseases. Other participants, while currently working in fields not directly focused on climate and health, expressed a desire to incorporate climate and health topics in future research. As one participant explained, I’m a researcher at an academic institution. Though my current research does not focus on climate change, I’m committed to ensuring my future research does.

Participants integrate climate into their curricula/teaching

Several participants reported incorporating climate and health topics in their educational offerings across various levels, including high school, undergraduate and graduate curricula. One participant shared, “”When I signed up for the certificate, I set an ambitious goal to create a climate & health program within our local health department and to teach a climate and health class at our local university. I’m proud to say I am spearheading both. The climate & health program is growing, and I currently teach a graduate level class on the environment & health.

In addition to formal instruction, one participant described developing educational opportunities for broader audiences, such as their workplace or community groups. The participant noted being “able to offer CC [climate change] education locally and internationally.”

Participants engage through volunteering and board service

Finally, participation in the certificate programme motivated many participants to engage differently in their workplaces, local communities or other organisations. This included being a part of workplace committees focused on climate, volunteering with climate-focused organisations and serving on advisory boards. One participant explained, ““Participating in this program inspired me to proactively seek out volunteer and board participation, for ventures promoting sustainable and regenerative practices.” Another participant described their experience engaging in climate and health at a professional level, I’ve been able to take a deeper dive into developing my professional association’s environmental catalyst group.”

Pivoters

Participants pivot their career paths and launch companies

Participants reported significant changes in their career paths, either securing new jobs or pivoting their roles at a current organisation, following the completion of the certificate programme. One participant shared, ““At the time I took the certificate course, I was working in leadership in a social services unit focused on childcare. Three months after completing the course, I accepted a position on our emergency preparedness team. I coordinate our medical reserve corp[s] and serve on the statewide extreme heat workgroup, among other roles.” Another participant reflected on a more substantial career shift stating, The certificate program was an incredibly important part of a career pivot from being a research coordinator at an integrative medicine clinic to now working as a policy analyst at a climate-focused NGO.” Another participant described a major career pivot, launching and now running a communication consultancy with an exclusive focus on climate issues.

Participants pursue further education

Participants have pursued further education in climate change and health, including advanced degrees, such as PhDs and MPHs, and specialised workshops. One participant reflected on a major career shift after completing the programme and pivoting to work full-time in the climate-health space, noting, “…It inspired me to do my MPH, for which I enrolled in the year after completing the Yale Climate Course… I was a primary care physician when I did the climate course; however, I am now a public health practitioner”

Climate communication

Both Integrators and Pivoters emerged as more proficient climate change and health communicators. Most participants reported increased confidence and awareness of topics in climate change and health, leading them to become more confident in talking with their family, friends and colleagues. One participant, who had worked as a community health advisor prior to the programme, shared that they strive to engage their networks in climate and health conversation, noting, “The course has helped me to start from simpler yet effective ways of working and knowing your audience before commencing.”

Participants also highlighted that the certificate programme improved their ability to communicate climate change more effectively and persuasively to broad audiences. One participant, who works for a digital media company, described, “The certificate program gave me a foundation from which to approach storytelling. My goal is to tell climate stories in a way that is engaging and personable rather than covering catastrophes, which is necessary and important, but readers increasingly want to be entertained. I hope to encourage people to be proactive about helping climate change through political engagement.”

Discussion

This study presents a comprehensive evaluation of an online Climate Change and Health Certificate programme using Kirkpatrick’s four-level model of training evaluation, with a focus on Levels 2–4. In the context of overall strong Level 2 (Learning and Self-Efficacy) outcomes, a meaningful gap emerged between learning (>90% of participants agreed or strongly agreed they understood four of six areas of knowledge queried) and self-efficacy competencies (only 70%–78% agreed or strongly agreed they had confidence in applying three of four skills queried). This gap represents a critical finding for professional education programmes. At Level 3 (Behaviour), the programme achieved impressive success in motivating behavioural change. An overwhelming 91.1% of participants reported being motivated or enabled to modify their work approach, 76.5% to pursue climate activities outside work, 58.7% to make career changes and 46.5% to pursue additional education. Our latent class analysis revealed two distinct behaviour change pathways: Integrators (50.2%), who embedded climate considerations into existing roles, and Pivoters (41.0%), who made substantial career or life transitions. Level 4 (Results) showed a 20.5 percentage-point increase in participants working in climate and health, from 45.4% before to 65.9% after the programme. Qualitative data revealed a diverse array of organisational and societal impacts, including the establishment of new climate change and health programming, curricular integration of climate change and health, and new community initiatives.

The knowledge–application gap: addressing the challenge

The most significant opportunity for improvement of the certificate programme is the relative deficit in self-efficacy in applied skills competencies at Level 2. This gap may constrain Level 3 behaviour transfer and Level 4 results. While participants feel equipped to understand and communicate about climate change, they are less confident in their ability to design and implement concrete solutions. This finding aligns with literature on the ‘know-do gap’ in professional education and suggests that even well-designed programmes struggle to build implementation skills through online delivery alone.15 16

This gap has several implications. First, it could explain why some participants remain Integrators rather than Pivoters—without confidence in applied skills, professionals may feel more comfortable enhancing existing work rather than launching new initiatives. Unfortunately, we did not ask survey questions to interrogate this systematically. Second, the gap may highlight the need for educational programmes to move beyond knowledge transfer to skill development through experiential learning, which has been noted as a need in other public health educational offerings.17 18 Third, the gap suggests that measuring learning outcomes solely through knowledge assessments may overestimate programme effectiveness.

Two pathways of professional impact

Our identification of Integrators and Pivoters provides a novel framework for understanding how professionals translate climate and health education into action. Integrators demonstrate that substantial impact can occur within existing roles—these participants brought climate perspectives to their current work, integrated content into teaching and research, and initiated workplace programmes. Their pathway shows how climate and health education can create distributed change across organisations and sectors.

Pivoters represent, potentially, a more transformative pathway, making significant life changes including career transitions, pursuing advanced degrees and launching climate-and-health-focused ventures. Both pathways are valuable and necessary for addressing the health impacts of climate change. Society will need both Integrators to weave their newfound knowledge into established roles and careers, and Pivoters to help build the ranks of new roles in climate change and health and to help develop the field. Educational programmes should recognise and support both pathways rather than privileging one over the other.

Implications for climate and health professional education

When analysed together through the lens of the Kirkpatrick model, our findings from the current follow-up study and our previous study of contemporaneous course evaluations6 offer several evidence-based principles for designing effective climate education programmes:

  1. Leverage peer learning: Our qualitative findings from our previous study consistently highlighted the value of cross-sector peer-to-peer learning.6 This suggests that cohort-based models with structured peer interaction enhance both learning and behaviour change.

  2. Address the application gap explicitly: Programmes must bridge the knowing-doing gap through applied exercises, training in implementation planning, skills practice and/or experiential learning. Online programmes may need to incorporate synchronous workshops, simulations and/or local practicum components.

  3. Design for multiple impact pathways: Recognising that professionals will apply learning differently based on their context, career stage and goals, resources and support should be provided for pathways for both Integrators and Pivoters.

  4. Focus measurement on behaviour and impact: While knowledge assessments are important, programmes should prioritise measuring Level 3 and 4 outcomes to understand true effectiveness.

  5. Build long-term support structures: The high rates of additional education and career changes suggest participants need ongoing support. Alumni networks, mentorship and/or continuing education opportunities could enhance impact.

Strengths and limitations

This study’s strengths include its theory-driven evaluation framework, mixed-methods approach and longitudinal follow-up at least 1 year postcompletion. The application of Kirkpatrick’s model provides a replicable framework for evaluating similar programmes and enables comparison across professional development contexts.

Several limitations warrant consideration. The 32% response rate raises concerns about selection bias, with more engaged or successful participants potentially overrepresented. Self-reported data may be subject to social desirability bias, particularly given participants’ investment in the programme. The absence of a control group limits causal inference about programme effects. Our Level 4 results measures remain limited, focusing primarily on individual career changes rather than broader organisational or societal outcomes. Further, the sample size limited our ability to identify predictors of different behaviour pathways or examine subgroup differences. The limited sample also makes it difficult to compare across the different iterations and updates to the programme over time. Relatedly, because we did not ask survey respondents to specify which year or cohort of the programme they participated in, we were unable to compare responses from cohorts 8 and 9, which were exposed to curricular updates, to responses from earlier cohorts. In addition, our evaluation of Level 2 learning was based on participants’ assessment of their knowledge and not on objective measures of knowledge. Moreover, respondents consisted predominantly of learners residing in high-income countries, with limited representation from low- and middle-income countries. As a result, findings may not fully capture the experiences of those in settings where climate-related health burdens are often greatest, and where resources to diminish those burdens are more limited. Also, the survey instrument was developed for this programme-specific evaluation and was not formally validated or pilot tested. However, items were grounded in established evaluation frameworks and the programme’s learning objectives, and the mixed-methods design enabled triangulation of findings across data sources. Finally, it should be noted that there have been critiques of Kirkpatrick’s model, with one such critique being that the model assumes that the levels are causally linked and positively correlated, which oversimplifies the complexity of learning, behaviour change and organisational outcomes.19

Opportunities

This evaluation suggests several priorities for future research and programme development. First, experimental or quasi-experimental designs could strengthen causal claims about programme impact. Second, developing validated instruments for measuring climate and health education outcomes across Kirkpatrick levels would enable programme comparison. Third, longitudinal tracking of participants could reveal how impacts evolve over time and whether behaviour changes persist. Fourth, research should examine which programme components most effectively bridge the knowledge–application gap. Finally, as behaviour is often shaped by individuals’ broader contexts, additional survey questions could explore persistent obstacles preventing participants from putting their knowledge into action.

Conclusion

Online professional education can achieve significant learning and self-efficacy, behaviour change and impact outcomes. However, the relative deficit in applied skills self-efficacy reveals a fundamental challenge in online professional education. By identifying two distinct pathways of behaviour change (Integrators and Pivoters) and documenting programmatic impacts across Kirkpatrick levels, this evaluation provides both evidence of effectiveness and clear directions for improvement. As the climate crisis intensifies, understanding how to effectively build professional capacity in climate change and health becomes increasingly critical. This study offers a model for rigorous evaluation while highlighting the continued need for innovation in translating knowledge into action.
